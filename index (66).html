Steel building pricing is primarily driven by width, roof style, and your local engineering requirements. Tap any factor to learn more.

Building width vs. length
Highest impact
Width is the biggest cost driver. Exceeding a standard 30-foot span requires a fundamental engineering change — from simple single-bar trusses to reinforced double truss or ladder leg designs — which can cause a price jump of 25–35%. Wider buildings also require heavier machinery to install (see Installation below).

Length, by contrast, is more cost-effective to add. Increasing the length of your building typically doesn't change the truss design, so you get more square footage for less money per foot compared to going wider.
Under 30ft wide → single truss designOver 30ft → double trusses / ladder legs+25–35% price jump at 31ft+
Roof style
High impact
Three styles, three price points. Regular / Standard — The most economical option, featuring rounded eaves. Good for simple storage.

A-Frame (Boxed Eve) — Mid-range pricing; panels run horizontally with a cleaner boxed corner look.

Vertical Roof — The most expensive and recommended for all buildings. Panels run vertically, allowing water and debris to shed effectively, preventing leaks and handling heavier loads.
Regular → most affordableBoxed Eve → mid-rangeVertical → best for 30ft+ & wet climates
Steel market & tariffs
External factor
Prices reflect global market conditions. Steel is a globally traded commodity — prices change daily based on supply, demand, and geopolitical events. Recent trade policies and tariffs (including those enacted in 2025–2026) have caused significant spikes in raw material costs that flow directly into building prices.

This is why locking in a quote sooner rather than later is often in your best interest. Prices quoted today may not be available in 60–90 days.
Steel prices fluctuate daily2025–2026 tariffs impacting costsLock in pricing when you're ready
Local engineering & wind/snow loads
High impact
Your zip code determines your requirements. Every building must be engineered to meet the wind speed and snow load ratings for its specific location. Higher load requirements mean upgrading from standard 14-gauge tubing to thicker 12-gauge, along with more frequent bracing — both of which add cost.

In Florida, many coastal counties also require Hi-Impact rated doors and windows, adding further to the total. This is determined at time of engineering, not at the time of quoting.
14-gauge → standard12-gauge → high wind/load areasHi-Impact required in most coastal FL counties
Foundation & site prep
Not included in price
Site work is separate — and often the biggest variable. Wider commercial-grade buildings often require specialized concrete footers or thicker monolithic slabs to support heavier trusses. The building price you see in the estimator does not include grading, clearing, drainage, or concrete work — these are contracted separately and vary significantly by property.
Concrete slab: ~$6–10/sq ftWider buildings → heavier foundation requirementsNot included in building quote
Installation & equipment
Medium impact
Wider buildings require specialized equipment. Standard tubular buildings under 30 feet can typically be erected by small crews without heavy machinery. Buildings over 30 feet wide — what we call "widespan" — require specialized equipment such as sky tracks or scissor lifts for safe installation. This equipment cost is factored into the installation price and is one reason the 30-foot threshold represents such a meaningful price jump.
Under 30ft → small crew, no heavy equipment31ft+ → sky track or scissor lift requiredEquipment cost included in installation

Why Steel

Six reasons steel wins. Every time.

The case for steel isn't sentiment — it's structural. Here's why our buildings outlast the cheap alternatives by decades.

01

180 MPH Wind Resistance

Engineered to meet Florida's strictest wind codes. When the next storm hits, your building stands.

Every StormSafe building is engineered to meet or exceed your local wind code — from standard 110 MPH ratings to 180+ MPH in coastal hurricane zones. We use 14-gauge primary framing with hurricane clips and a continuous load path from roof to foundation. Anchor bolts, brace patterns, and connection design are spec'd to your site's exposure category. The result: a structure rated to withstand winds that would tear apart a wood-frame building.

Learn more
02

Fully Galvanized Steel

Zinc-coated at the molecular level. No rust, no corrosion, no maintenance headaches — ever.

All structural steel and roof panels are hot-dip galvanized — bonded to zinc through an 850°F bath that creates a protective barrier at the molecular level. Unlike painted steel that chips and fades, galvanized steel self-heals minor scratches through cathodic protection. In coastal salt-air environments, galvanized buildings outlast painted alternatives by 3–5x. You will never repaint or treat for rust.

Learn more
03

Fire Resistant

Steel doesn't burn. While wood-frame structures ignite, your building holds its ground.

Steel has a Class A fire rating — the highest available. It won't ignite, won't fuel a fire, and won't add combustible material. Wood-frame buildings lose 80% of their load-bearing capacity at 600°F; steel holds structural integrity to over 1100°F. For commercial & agricultural buildings, this often translates to 20–40% lower fire insurance premiums. In wildfire-prone areas, your steel structure also acts as a firebreak between neighboring wood buildings.

Learn more
04

Termite Proof

Wood feeds termites. Steel starves them. Zero risk of structural damage from insects.

Termites cause over $5 billion in damage each year in the US — and wood-frame buildings need treatment every 5–7 years. Steel offers nothing for termites, carpenter ants, powder-post beetles, or any wood-boring insect. You will never schedule a treatment, never inspect for damage, never replace structural members eaten from the inside. In Florida and the Southeast where aggressive Formosan termites are common, this is one of the largest hidden savings of going steel.

Learn more
05

50+ Year Lifespan

A properly installed steel building outlasts wood by decades. This is the last building you'll need.

Wood-frame buildings typically need major structural work at 25–30 years and full replacement around 40–50. Galvanized steel buildings routinely last 60–80+ years with no structural intervention. StormSafe builds come standard with a 50-year structural warranty on the frame and a 40-year paint warranty on the panels. That makes the building a multi-generational asset — and on a per-year basis, one of the cheapest construction options available.

Learn more
06

Lower Long-Term Cost

No termite treatment. No rot repair. No re-roofing every 15 years. Steel pays for itself.

Look past the upfront price: wood-frame buildings average $1,800–$3,500/year in maintenance (paint, seal, termite treatment, re-roofing, board replacement). A comparable steel building averages $50–$150/year, mostly occasional power-washing. Over 30 years, that's $50K–$100K+ in maintenance savings. Insurance premiums run 15–25% lower for steel due to wind/fire/pest resistance, and steel buildings consistently appraise higher than wood equivalents at resale.

Frequently Asked

Answers to the big questions

Everything customers ask before pulling the trigger. Tap any question.

Do I need a foundation? Who builds it?

Yes — every steel building needs a foundation. Most residential garages and small commercial buildings sit on a concrete slab (4–6″ thick) with engineered anchor bolt placements. Larger structures may need pier-and-grade-beam or full perimeter footings.

We provide the engineered foundation drawings with your building plans, and you have two options: hire a local concrete contractor (we can recommend trusted ones), or roll site prep + foundation into your quote and we'll coordinate the entire package end to end.

What permits do I need? Do you help?

Almost every county and municipality requires a building permit for steel structures over a certain size (typically 100–200 sq ft). Some coastal counties also require hurricane impact certifications and special wind load engineering.

We provide all the documentation you need: stamped engineered drawings, foundation plans, wind load calculations, and material specifications. You (or your contractor) submit them to your local building department. If you'd rather we handle the permit application directly, we offer that as a service — just ask.

Can I finance my steel building?

StormSafe doesn't offer in-house financing, but most customers fund their build through their own bank or credit union. Common options include home equity loans, HELOCs, personal construction loans, and unsecured personal loans.

Use the Financing Calculator in our Plan Your Build tool above to get a monthly payment estimate — bring that number to your lender as a starting point. Many of our customers qualify for the best rates by using their home's equity since the structure adds permanent property value.

Is the building delivered as a kit, or fully installed?

Both options are available. Most customers choose full delivery & installation — our crew arrives with the building, sets it up on your prepared foundation, and hands you the keys.

If you'd rather erect it yourself (or hire your own crew), we can deliver as a complete building kit with all framing, panels, fasteners, sealant, and assembly instructions included. Kits save 15–25% on the total cost but require comparable experience to assemble safely. Talk to a building specialist about which option fits your situation.

What's covered under the warranty?

Every StormSafe building comes with:

• 50-year structural warranty on the primary steel frame against rust-through, corrosion failure, or load-bearing failure.
• 40-year paint warranty on roof & wall panels against fade, chalk, and chip.
• 20-year warranty on roll-up doors, walk doors, and windows against mechanical failure.
• Lifetime workmanship guarantee on our installation work — if something we built fails, we fix it.

Warranties transfer to the next owner if you sell the property.

Can I add doors and windows wherever I want?

Yes — within structural limits. Roll-up doors, walk doors, and windows can be placed on any wall, in nearly any position, as long as we maintain minimum corner clearances (1′ on eave sides, 2–3′ on gable ends depending on door height) and 1′ minimum spacing between adjacent openings for header support.

The Customize tool on our site lets you place doors and windows visually on each wall and shows you in real time if something won't fit. For complex layouts or unusual placements, talk to a building specialist — there are creative solutions for most situations.
